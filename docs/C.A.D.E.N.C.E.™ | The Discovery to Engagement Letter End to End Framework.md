What “Qualified Opportunity” Means (Advisory Standard)
Inside this OS, a qualified opportunity is defined a person/entity with a problem and is:

Financially able
Operationally serviceable (or “sizable and serviceable,” where appropriate to their model)
Genuinely interested in exploring a solution
Notice what’s not in that definition: “has the final decision-maker on the line.” Often you (and your clients) will be one title south and must champion up. We keep aiming up-chain, but we credit opportunities that meet the three core boxes. Encourage clients to adopt this same definition so approvals don’t stall behind title fetishism; what matters is fit + intent and a pathway to leadership. (When appropriate, this can inform how milestone credit is recognized inside the engagement language.)

Phase 1 - Immediate Post-Discovery
Objective: Maintain momentum the moment the call ends; establish continuity and control of next steps.

1.0 Timing Standard (tight, context-led)

T+0–5 min: Export the Fathom transcript. Note meeting title, date/time, attendees, and any verbatim “next-step” phrasing the exec used.
T+15–30 min: Send the Post-Discovery Immediate Email (short, crisp, transcript-aware).
Within 24 hrs: Begin SoW creation (Phase 2) via the transcript-driven SoW/EL SOP (do not hand-craft giant proposals).
Call Two cadence (contextual):
If they are scheduled for a review call during Call One: reference the date/time in your emails after discovery and before the SoW email - leading into Call 2 “review” meetings.
If they didn’t schedule and gave no timing preference (Always push for call 2, you must train this muscle), you may suggest review when appropriate - not in every variation of the first email. Keep fishing for call 2 aligned with the timing they requested when they rejected the booking on Call 1.
Why this sequence: You’re signaling continuity, not reselling. Light email → rigorous SoW. This prevents going dark, over-talking mechanics, or sending bloated “proposals” that feel vendorish.

This email does four things (and nothing else):

CTA Example for the post discovery/pre SoW email.

Mirrors their scheduling posture: if they already defined timing for a review, align to that (don’t push generic “Call Two” if they said “when the SoW lands, we’ll do a review meeting”).

Email closes deals. Treat this first note as the first brick of the close, not a fluffy recap. Most engagements finalize over email when you keep momentum and posture.


1.2 Post-Discovery Objection Posture (quality/“will this go anywhere?”)

If meeting quality comes up after discovery, it’s a signal you didn’t lock risk and qualification clearly. Dissolve it by calmly restating:


Our qualified-op standard = financially able + (operationally/sizable) serviceable + genuinely interested.
Titles: we always aim for the highest relevant executive, but at times, wins are champion-driven one title south; find out if they’ve ever had to push proposals up-chain. (Have them break down the process)
Risk posture: the engagement formalizes clear outcomes with milestone structure, so worst-case the client still wins (details live in SoW → EL, not in this email).
Keep it short; save the heavier articulation for SoW/EL.

The Advisory QOE™ Doctrine
Risk, Value, and Qualification at the Point of Decision
Quick Side Monologue


Purpose
To harden your posture when value is challenged, clarify your stance on negotiation, and refine how you interpret objections that are often projections of internal weakness rather than true resistance. At high-ticket levels, pricing objections, quality concerns, and delays rarely reveal you-they expose them.
 

This doctrine ensures that you can identify which is which, disqualify decisively, and remain in absolute control of value integrity.


QOE-1. The Law of Non-Negotiation
“There are three parties you never negotiate with: terrorists, children and prospects.” - Termsheetinator


Negotiation at this level is not compromise-it is corrosion. When you reduce price without significantly reducing value, you are teaching the buyer that your margin was artificial and your conviction is conditional. The governing rule is Price follows value, never precedes it.

If you must reduce price, you reduce scope far more drastically than cost, so the comparison becomes absurd.


Example:
An engagement priced at $50,000 may be reduced to $25,000, but the $25,000 version should remove 80% of the deliverable value, control, and intensity.


The choice should feel self-correcting. The contrast preserves your positioning and forces the client to self-select credibility.


Framework:

Your Price / Your Terms = Full value, full control, premium experience.
Their Price / Your Terms = Extremely reduced scope, no strategic layer, tactical only.
Your Price / Their Terms = Selective accommodation, not concession.
Every concession must carry a visible cost. Otherwise, you’ve trained the market to negotiate with your weakness instead of your strength.






QOE-2. Quality of Engagement (QOE™)
(Derived from “Quality of Earnings” in commercial finance)


Purpose:
To evaluate and maintain the integrity of your advisory relationships over time. QOE™ measures the health, clarity, and maturity of your engagements - the internal benchmark that determines whether the way you work matches the value you command.


Definition:
QOE™ = The degree to which your advisory process remains structured, respected, and value-dense from first interaction to close.


Indicators of High QOE™:

Every engagement follows defined cadence and communication discipline.
Clients respond with predictability, not volatility.
Deliverables are aligned with documented expectations.
Scope changes are formalized, never casual.
The relationship feels like leadership, not labor.
Indicators of Low QOE™:

Reactive communication and undefined checkpoints.
Constant justification of your worth.
Repeated attempts by clients to compress cost or expand scope.
Lack of mutual clarity on next steps or outcomes.
Operational Use:
QOE™ is reviewed internally. It answers one question: Are we operating as premium advisors or reactive vendors? When QOE™ is high, perception of value rises automatically - removing downward pressure on price and positioning.



QOE-3. Qualified Opportunity Anchor (QOA™)

Purpose:
To confirm and anchor an opportunity as genuinely qualified before committing resources or closing transactions.


While QOE™ governs how you engage, QOA™ governs what you engage with. It’s the closing instrument that protects you from entering mandates that cannot convert.


Definition:
A Qualified Opportunity Anchor (QOA™) is the confirmation mechanism that allows both you and the client to validate that the prospect or company meets your Qualified Opportunity Criteria.


A Qualified Opportunity is a person or entity that is:


Problem-Aware – They recognize the pain or performance gap your solution addresses.
Financially Able – The investment is a decision of confidence, not strain.
Operationally Serviceable – They have infrastructure or capacity to integrate what you deliver.
Sizable & Serviceable – The scale of the opportunity justifies advisory involvement; it’s not a micro-transaction.
Genuinely Interested – They show behavioral proof of engagement, not just verbal interest.
Note: Decision-making capability is not a qualifying variable-it’s a directional one.
You may be working one title south of the executive signer; that’s acceptable if the champion is credible and connected.


Usage Context:

The QOA™ is a closing anchor, not a discovery tool.
It is introduced only when the advisory scope, pricing, and structure are clear.
The client uses it post-call to validate that a prospect fits your qualification framework.




Why It Matters: 

Clients often default to lowering cost when they can’t control quality or convert demand. The QOA™ shifts responsibility back where it belongs - to their process, not your price. It makes clear that:

You generate demand.
They must process and convert it.
The qualification of a lead is their validation checkpoint, not your vulnerability.
Operational Guidelines:

Treat the QOA™ as a high-risk anchor - an “ace in your back pocket.”
Deploy only when you sense real hesitation on scope or value.
The document itself outlines your qualification framework and allows the client to apply it.
The goal is to bring logic and structure into a moment that would otherwise be emotional or fearful.
Positioning Language:
“You’ll use this to confirm that what we’re bringing to you fits the type of client or prospect you actually want - someone with a real problem, financially able, operationally sized to fit your capacity, and genuinely interested. It’s a quick validation anchor that protects both sides.”


Interpretation:
When the QOA™ reveals a mismatch, the issue is not your cost or quality - it’s their target clarity or conversion confidence. That’s a disqualifier, not an objection.

5.6.2 If You’re Working Through a Champion
A Champion is your internal representative - they carry your work upward.

Their two invisible battles:

Credibility: They must sound like they’re bringing in something valuable, not risky.
Momentum: They must navigate bureaucracy that moves slower than they do.
You win not by making them sound persuasive, but by making them look organized. That means you give them structure - not scripts.











How to Think and Create for Champions

Start with Their Internal Audience in Mind.
 Before you write or speak, ask: Who are they talking to internally?
What does that person value - cost control, optics, or growth?
What would make your Champion look sharp and prepared when forwarding your note?
What would make them feel safe sending it?

→ This defines your vocabulary and rhythm.
You’re writing for the person reading through them, not for them.

Simplify the Narrative Chain.
Champions rarely forward complexity.
If your SoW is 10 pages, mentally compress it to one strategic point per layer: What outcome, by when, under what guardrail?
Strip adjectives, remove jargon, and anchor every point to a tangible business motion (revenue, risk, or resource).
→ When in doubt, design clarity that can be defended by someone who wasn’t in the call.
Translate Value to Institutional Language.
Champions think in enthusiasm; leaders think in systems.
Convert enthusiasm into structured logic:
Instead of “great results,” think: “this structure replaces variable outcomes with measurable progress.”
Instead of “we’ll grow faster,” think: “this creates bandwidth without headcount.”
→ Build phrasing that would survive a boardroom without you present.
Design Forwardability, Not Personality.
Your goal is to create content that travels upward intact.
Ask yourself: If my name were removed from this message, would it still make sense?
Avoid tone markers that tie it to “you.”
Make it stand alone - neat, factual, clean.
→ The strongest advisory messages can survive decontextualization.
End With Permission, Not Pressure.
Champions move faster when they don’t feel burdened.
So you close with light permission - phrases that let them use or ignore what you’ve built without losing dignity.
(“Use this if helpful for internal review.” “Ignore if this is already covered.”)
This creates a low-friction environment where your Champion feels enabled, not enlisted.


5.6.3 If You’re Working Directly With a Decision-Maker
A Decision-Maker already controls the outcome - they don’t need validation, they need efficiency.

This path requires you to stop “selling logic” and start removing resistance.


The thinking pattern is opposite from Champion work:

Less education, more confirmation.
Less proof, more trust.
Less narrative, more clarity.
How to Think and Create for Decision-Makers
Recognize What They Already Know.
Decision-Makers usually know the risk, reward, and cost.
Don’t repeat what’s known.
Ask yourself: What detail or ambiguity could slow signature or payment?
Timing?
Contract mechanics?
Risk optics?
Focus your writing on resolving those variables.
Audit for Friction, Not Persuasion.
The deal is already emotionally sold.
At this stage, friction hides in details.
Ask: What could make this feel administratively heavy or uncertain?
Then design your communication to dissolve that friction.
Examples of friction types:
Ambiguous milestone definition → adds legal review time.
Unclear payment flow → adds finance delay.
Overly casual tone → reduces trust in delivery rigor.
→ Clean those out before you send anything.
Signal Readiness, Not Urgency.
Decision-Makers interpret tone as risk calibration.
“Urgent” = desperate.
“Ready” = controlled.
So when crafting your message, imagine they’re testing: Does this person look like they can handle our scale?
The way to pass that test: your words read calm, measured, certain - never rushed.






Formalize Immediately When Invited.
The biggest mistake is waiting too long after “we’re good.”
The longer the lag between verbal approval and paperwork, the higher the risk of inertia.
Aim to send the Engagement Letter or invoice within 24 hours.
Keep tone concise: confidence replaces ceremony.
Reinforce Logic, Don’t Repeat It.
Once they’ve said yes in principle, your job is to reconfirm why their decision was rational - not resell the scope.
Mentally frame it as: “We both agreed this path de-risks the outcome; now let’s capture that agreement formally.”
That’s how advisors close without sounding transactional.

6.1 The Four Axes Diagnostic (quick self-check before any move)

Economy: Is this Old (formal cadence, documented approvals) or New (fast, operator-led)?
Tempo: Fast / Moderate / Slow-Political (from D.E.D.I.™).

6.2 The Micro-Step Loop (your invariant process)
Observe the last signal (email content, timing, tone).
Hypothesize the internal state (review stage, blocker, energy).
Ask one sharp question that forces a real read (feedback rope or approval rope).

Repeat. This loop is your pipeline motion.







6.3 The Two Ropes (what every micro-step must serve)

Use mapping questions to reveal who reviews, when, and what they need to see.
If a move doesn’t serve one or both ropes, it’s noise.

Capacity/Timing: quarter-close, resource locks, vendor sprawl. → Solve with start-date clarity and “day-one” readiness signals.
Micro-step rule: choose one friction, neutralize one friction.
